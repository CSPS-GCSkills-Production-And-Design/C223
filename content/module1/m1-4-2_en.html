CLCA obligations

Identifying modern treaty/CLCA obligations

To help the business owner refine the requirement and ensure upcoming procurement activities are fulfilled in accordance with applicable obligations, it is crucial to know how to identify and understand them. Thankfully, resources exist to help navigate this step.

Click on a resource to learn how it can help identify and understand applicable modern treaty/CLCAs obligations.

Accordion

  • Indigenous Services Canada (ISC) or departmental Indigenous procurement teams

These teams will be able to list applicable procurement obligations and provide advice on how to meet them. 

  • The Consultation and Information Service (CIS)

CIS provides information on other established and potential Aboriginal and Treaty rights that might affect your procurement activities.

  • Section 9.35 of the Supply Manual

Both you and your client should familiarize yourselves with modern treaties/CLCAs and their procurement obligations. Most of them are listed in that section of the Supply Manual.

  • This course!

While it was designed for contracting officers, a lot of the information contained in this course is also relevant for business owners. Do not hesitate to recommend it to them.

Let’s have a closer look at those obligations.

2.5.3.1.  Legal duty to consult

Obligation overview

As some government activities may adversely impact potential or established Indigenous or Treaty rights, business owners are responsible to assess and, if required, to consult with Indigenous groups that may be affected.  The outcome of these consultations might impact the definition of the requirement and resulting procurement activities.

Consultation activities should take place prior to the business owner sending the requisition to you, as consultation and accommodation may impact the procurement strategy as a whole, including the statement of work, the procurement timeline, funding, approval and even the decision to proceed with the proposed activity.

What does it mean for you?

Make sure the need for Indigenous consultation and accommodation has been assessed at the requirement definition step by the business owner and be informed of consultation outcomes. This is an opportunity to learn about issues, complaints and local needs and context that will inform your procurement strategy.

For large-scale projects, the earlier you are involved, the greater your ability to:

  • address procurement questions and concerns and
  • mitigate downstream issues.

What might you learn from consultations with Indigenous peoples?

Hunting rights or a wildlife conservation could be impacted by procurements such as the construction and use of snowmobile trails. To retain access to their hunting grounds and to maintain a viable wildlife population, Indigenous communities may suggest diverging the snowmobile trail to a different area of their traditional territory.

Applying the duty to consult

The Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs Canada (CIRNAC) has established consultation protocols with a number of Indigenous groups across Canada. To ensure a coordinated, efficient and effective consultation process, business owners must contact the CIS team prior to engaging in consultation activities.

Note that the scope of the required consultations can vary significantly. The nature of the right and the severity of the potential harm of a Crown decision or action on the Indigenous or treaty right will determine the level of consultation required.

For practical advice and guidance in determining when the duty to consult may arise and how it may be fulfilled, consult the following Guidelines: Guidelines for Federal Officials to Fulfill the Duty to Consult

2.5.3.2.  Right of first refusal

Obligation overview

Some Modern Treaties (CLCAs) require departments and agencies to “first solicit bids from within the applicable land claim area” or to provide “right of first refusal,” “first priority,” “preferential treatment” or “first consideration” to land claims beneficiary businesses for requirements related to parks, heritage sites, silviculture and conservation areas.   

While there may be an obligation to limit bids to beneficiary businesses, note that the application of this obligation remains conditional upon the supplier meeting the department’s operational requirements. If there are no beneficiary businesses that can provide the sought-after requirements, full consideration can be given to bidders based outside of the applicable treaty area.

Seek guidance from ISC or your departmental Indigenous procurement team

The terms used and modern treaty/CLCA application differ from treaty to treaty, that is why it is important to interpret them in the context of the clause they are found. Consult an Indigenous procurement advisor to confirm how this obligation should be applied in the context of your procurement.

2.5.3.3.  Economic measures

Obligation overview

Procurement related to certain topics have provisions requiring the inclusion of economic measures in the bid evaluation criteria. The purpose of their inclusion is to increase opportunities for modern treaty/CLCA beneficiaries to benefit from procurement.  

Specifically, what does it mean?

The bid evaluation criteria must require bidders to include an Indigenous Benefit Plan (IBP) as part of their proposal. The IBP must demonstrate their use of economic measures such as Indigenous  labour, engagement of Indigenous professional services, or use of Indigenous firms, in carrying out the work outlined in the request for proposals.

-          Contacting ISC to determine the applicable economic measures to include in the solicitation document.

-          Determining how they will be evaluated.

Ensure socio-economic criteria are part of the requirement and help your client identify or refine the socio-economic evaluation criteria if necessary.

Existence of head offices, administrative offices or facilities: Having a head office, administrative offices or facility within the modern treaty/CLCA area.

-      A description of the number, type and size of the head offices, administrative offices or other facilities that are located in the modern treaty/CLCA area.

2.5.3.5.  Access permits

Obligation overview

Some modern treaties/CLCAs contain provisions related to access to the area covered by the treaty. This may include obtaining licences or permits and complying with conditions such as reporting on usage of natural resources by the contractor.

-          Ensure other access conditions are fulfilled.

2.5.3.6.  Knowledge of and experience with Indigenous people

Obligation overview

Some modern treaties/CLCAs have an obligation requiring federal contractors operating within the land claim area to have knowledge of or experience working with the local Indigenous population. This provision is common for modern treaties/CLCAs in the Yukon, especially with regards to procurements related to parks, heritage sites, wildlife and archaeology.

The Teslin Tlingit Council Final Agreement for example, has a provision requiring contractors to have special knowledge or experience of the Teslin Tlingit people when a procurement is taking place in a Designated Heritage Site. The inclusion of this type of criterion is important because designated heritage sites are directly related to efforts to preserve Teslin Tlingit’s history and culture.

What does it mean for you?

Ensure solicitation documents and evaluation criteria meet appropriate obligations and statements.

2.5.3.7.  Unbundling

Obligation overview

Many contracts are essentially a set of requirements, bundled up under a single contract. Under several modern treaties/CLCAs, business owners and contracting authorities may unbundle their procurement into separate contracts so smaller Indigenous business suppliers may bid on them.

This is a great opportunity to put the market research results to good use. As you determine how to structure the procurement, consider the local capacity and the number of qualified Indigenous businesses that are available to submit a bid.

Give me an example

There is a project to construct a series of buildings in a modern treaty/CLCA area. As part of the project, the roads and foundation platforms will need to be put in place before the buildings are constructed. This project could be unbundled into separate procurements, as such, have a contract for building the roads, the foundations, and the buildings.

What does it mean for you?

Work with the business owner to separate requirements into commodity or geographic groupings to allow smaller and more specialized Indigenous businesses to submit a bid. The result of the process may be that, instead of having one contract, there may now be several smaller ones.

2.5.3.8.  Structure of the requirement

Obligation overview

Most modern treaties/CLCAs require that contracts are structured in a way that provides all reasonable opportunities for beneficiary businesses to submit competitive bids. That means working with the business owner to ensure that:

  • elements such as qualifications, certifications and security requirements are not artificially inflated and are essential to the fulfillment of the contract
  • timeframes and schedules take the local context into account.

Give me an example

The James Bay region of Quebec celebrates an event called the Goose Break. Each spring, schools and Cree businesses shut their doors for a few weeks and the community heads out on the land to hunt returning geese. In this context, providing all reasonable opportunities for beneficiary businesses to submit competitive bid means taking this event into account when establishing timelines and deliverables.

Getting the most out of engagement sessions

The intelligence gathered through the consultation and engagement sessions can provide cultural awareness and useful feedback from Indigenous communities regarding local context. Knowledge of

environmental issues, safety factors, local holiday, hunting seasons, weather and industry days is key in developing the procurement strategy.  This information will help in assessing the impacts local context has on the solicitation process and on the delivery of the work under any subsequent contract.

2.5.3.9.  Notification

Obligation overview

A common obligation is the requirement to provide notification of the procurement to the applicable  land claimant groups.

This obligation might not impact the requirement definition, however, it is still important to identify and understand it early so you can plan your procurement activities accordingly. Go to the Bid solicitation section of this module for more information about this obligation.
